Big question

As AI enters expert decision spaces globally...

Does cultural background shape whether people defer to AI systems or human experts when the two directly conflict?

Healthcare

AI diagnostic tools used in radiology and cardiology across North America and Latin America. Mexico is an active deployment site today (Global Health Intelligence, 2024).

Employment

AI systems screen resumes, recommend candidates, and guide hiring decisions in companies across dozens of countries.

Education

AI tutors and grading tools are used in classrooms from the US to Brazil to South Korea, often with minimal cultural adaptation.

Specific research question

Does power distance moderate deference?

Does cultural background, Mexico vs. United States, influence whether individuals defer to AI or human expert recommendations when the two conflict, and is this effect driven by perceived legitimacy or authority rather than perceived competence?

Background - Account 1

Deference is about competence.

The dominant framework: people follow whoever they believe is more accurate, not based on source type.

Hou & Jung (2021) - When AI and humans disagreed, people followed whoever they thought was more accurate. Source type did not matter.
Logg et al. (2019) - When AI was clearly better, people preferred it. Accuracy is what drove the choice.
Dietvorst et al. (2015) - After seeing AI make one mistake, people avoided it, even when it was still more accurate overall.
Skitka et al. (1999) - Under pressure, people sometimes over-trusted automated systems. Again, it was about perceived reliability.

People evaluate who is more likely to be correct and follow that source.

Key prediction: No cultural difference. Both groups follow whoever seems more accurate, regardless of background.
Background - Account 2

Deference is about authority.

A competing account: people may not only ask who is accurate. They may also ask who has the recognized role, credentials, and accountability to advise.

LEGITIMATE
AUTHORITY

Veiga et al. (2001) - In settings where hierarchy matters, new technology may be resisted because it does not fit the expected authority role, not only because it seems inaccurate.

Wang (2025) - Across 20 countries, high power-distance contexts showed lower AI acceptance even when perceived accuracy was considered.

Authority logic - A human expert can carry legitimacy through professional role, credentials, and accountability. An AI system may have a track record, but it does not occupy the same social role.

Why power distance?

Why power distance?

Power distance captures how cultures evaluate authority, which is the mechanism this study is trying to isolate.

United States

40

Lower power distance

Mexico

81

Higher power distance

Hofstede (1980): Power distance describes how much a culture accepts unequal authority relationships. Mexico scores higher than the US, making this a useful contrast.

In higher power-distance contexts, formal role, credentials, and seniority may matter more when deciding who has authority to advise.

An AI system can be perceived as competent, but it may not fit the culturally expected role of a legitimate advisor.

A Mexico vs. US difference alone would not prove power distance. The stronger test is whether perceived legitimacy explains the cultural difference in deference.

Why Mexico and the United States?

This comparison gives us the clearest test.

Largest neighboring PD gap

Mexico scores 81, the US scores 40 on Hofstede's scale - one of the largest gaps between neighboring countries, replicated across decades. Strong signal to detect an effect.

Controls confounds better

Unlike East Asian comparisons, Mexico does not differ strongly from the US on long-term orientation. Religiosity can be measured and statistically controlled rather than confounded (Wang, 2025).

Ecologically valid

US-built AI tools are actively deployed in Mexican healthcare today (Global Health Intelligence, 2024). This is not a hypothetical - it is a real deployment situation that already exists.

Gap in the literature

The missing piece is the mechanism.

AI deference studies

Prior work often tests whether perceived competence predicts following AI or humans, but cultural background is usually not the central comparison.

Cross-cultural AI studies

Cross-cultural work can show differences in AI acceptance, but it often relies on attitudes rather than direct choices when AI and a human expert conflict.

This study

Addresses this gap by measuring advisor choice, perceived competence, and perceived legitimacy in the same AI-human conflict task.

Sample and recruitment

Sample plan required by the proposal

Target N

N = 240 total

120 per country. Expected final usable sample around 200 after exclusions (100 per country).

US participants

ASU SONA or online pool. US residents, English fluent, no professional AI or ML expertise.

Mexico participants

Spanish-language university networks or online recruitment. Mexico residents, Spanish fluent, no professional AI or ML expertise.

Data collection

Four counterbalanced scenarios. Two technical domains and two interpersonal domains.

Translation and controls

Forward-back translation. Covariates include religiosity, AI familiarity, education, and age.

Exclusions and covariates

Data quality and what we control for.

Exclusion criteria

Participant level: failed attention check, completion under 5 min, professional AI/ML expertise, country mismatch.
Trial level: conflict perception below 4. After each scenario participants rate "To what extent did the sources disagree?" (1-7). Trials below 4 are dropped - the conflict condition was not met.
Sensitivity check: all primary analyses repeated for trials scoring 6 or higher only.

Covariates - included in all models

Religiosity - Wang (2025) found this independently predicts AI acceptance. 2-item average, z-scored. Isolates it from the cultural group effect.
AI familiarity - Regular AI users may respond differently. Single 7-pt item, z-scored.
Education - May shape how people evaluate technical sources. Ordinal, z-scored.
Age - Different baseline attitudes toward AI and authority. Z-scored.

Pre-scenario measures

Existing beliefs, measured before any scenario.

AI Authority Perception Scale - 3 items, 7-point agreement

"AI systems can be legitimate authorities in professional decision-making."
"I would consider an AI system's recommendation to carry the same weight as a human expert's."
"AI systems are tools that assist humans rather than authorities that direct them." (reverse scored)

Score = average after reverse scoring. Higher = greater perceived AI authority.

Authority Criteria Scale - 4 items, 7-point importance

"The source has formal credentials or professional qualifications."
"The source has a proven track record of accuracy." (performance subscale)
"The source is accountable to others for the consequences of its recommendations."
"The source is human." (social authority subscale)

Social authority subscale = items 1, 3, 4. Performance subscale = item 2 alone.

2

Mechanism test

Question: Does legitimacy explain choice beyond competence?

Analysis: logistic mixed-effects regression

DV: advisor choice

Predictors: legitimacy rating, competence rating, country, country × legitimacy, country × competence

Random effects: participant and scenario

Expected: legitimacy predicts choice, especially for Mexico.

3

Source of legitimacy

Question: Does culture predict legitimacy ratings?

Analysis: linear mixed-effects model

DV: relative legitimacy rating

Predictors: country, domain, country × domain, covariates

Random effects: participant and scenario

Expected: Mexico rates the human expert as more legitimate.

Alternative outcome

What if competence explains everything?

The pattern

Both groups pick whoever they rated as more competent. Legitimacy does not explain additional cultural differences. Cultural difference in deference disappears in the actual decision moment.

Note: This would NOT mean culture is irrelevant. Wang (2025) found survey-level skepticism in high-PD countries - but survey skepticism may not carry over to live decisions.

What it means

For theory: Hou and Jung's (2021) competence account generalizes across cultures. The Western framework holds in a high-PD context - a meaningful positive finding.

For practice: When deploying AI in Mexico, what matters is whether AI seems accurate and reliable - not how it is introduced or who endorses it.

Interpreting support

The authority account would be supported by a pattern, not just one result.

Limitations

What this study cannot fully settle

Scenario-based choices

Participants respond to hypothetical scenarios, so choices may differ from real high-stakes decisions.

Unequal stakes across domains

Medical decisions may feel more serious than career or interpersonal decisions, which could affect deference independently of culture.

Two-country comparison

Mexico and the US provide a useful contrast, but results would not automatically generalize to all high- and low-power-distance cultures.
